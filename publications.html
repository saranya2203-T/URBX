Executive Summary

The Repetition Paradox in Urban Planning

Urban planning systems repeatedly diagnose the same problems—housing shortages, congestion, spatial inequality, environmental degradation—yet reproduce remarkably similar outcomes across cities and decades. This research identifies and conceptualizes this phenomenon as the Repetition Paradox: a condition where planning institutions continuously generate plans, policies, and projects that acknowledge systemic failures but remain structurally incapable of learning from them. The study argues that the persistence of planning failures is not primarily a lack of data, expertise, or intent. Instead, it stems from institutional repetition—the reuse of inherited frameworks, standardized indicators, and procedural templates that prioritize compliance and continuity over reflection and adaptation. Even when new technologies, datasets, or participatory tools are introduced, they are absorbed into existing decision-making logics, limiting their transformative potential. Through analytical examination of planning processes and policy cycles, the research demonstrates how repetition is embedded at multiple levels: problem framing, spatial classification, funding allocation, and project evaluation. This leads to cities planning for change while structurally ensuring sameness. The Repetition Paradox reframes urban planning failure as a governance and intelligence problem rather than a technical one. It highlights the need for planning systems that can retain institutional memory, evaluate past outcomes spatially, and actively disrupt inherited decision patterns. This research contributes a conceptual foundation for planning intelligence systems that move beyond optimization and prediction, toward learning, accountability, and structural change in urban decision-making.
